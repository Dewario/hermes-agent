# Washington State Civil Rules (CR) — Discovery Citation Verification (2026)

**Scope:** Plaintiff-side legal discovery skill. Verifying that each cited Washington
Superior Court Civil Rule (CR) is still good law as of 2026, with exact current
citations, 2024–2026 amendment notes, and confirmation of specific factual premises.

**Jurisdiction note:** Washington does **not** use the Federal Rules of Civil
Procedure (FRCP). It has its own **Superior Court Civil Rules (CR)**, promulgated
statewide by the Washington Supreme Court and supplemented by county-level Local
Civil Rules (LCR).

**Primary source:** Washington State Courts, official rule PDFs at
`www.courts.wa.gov/court_rules/` (Superior Court Civil Rules set), Supreme Court
rule-adoption orders, and Westlaw/WSBA secondary confirmation. Rule text on
courts.wa.gov is current with amendments received through at least March 1, 2026.

**Verification date:** 2026-07-19.

**Legend for `needs_attorney_rule_confirm`:** `true` = a licensed WA attorney should
confirm before relying (usually because the point is county-local, discretionary,
fact-specific, or a jurisdiction-specific trap). `false` = statewide rule text is
clear and directly verified against the official source.

---

## Executive summary of key confirmations

- **All listed rules (CR 26, 30, 31, 33, 34, 36, 37, 45) remain good law in 2026.** None
  were repealed. Three were amended in 2024 (CR 26, CR 30, CR 45).
- **WA does NOT have FRCP-style mandatory initial disclosures.** CR 26(a) is titled
  "Discovery Methods" — it lists the discovery devices; it is not a disclosure
  obligation. There is no CR 26(a)(1) initial-disclosure duty.
- **CR 33 does NOT impose a 25-interrogatory limit.** The stated premise ("WA CR 33 has
  a 25 limit") is **incorrect** — that is the *federal* rule (FRCP 33(a)(1)). WA CR 33
  imposes **no statewide numerical cap**; limits come from county **local** rules
  (e.g., King County 40 including discrete subparts; Pierce County 25/35 by track).
- **CR 26(b)(1) does NOT contain the federal 2015 proportionality standard.** WA still
  uses the older "relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action"
  scope language. It has pre-2015-style court-imposed limits in CR 26(b)(1)(A)–(C)
  (cumulative/duplicative; ample opportunity; burden vs. benefit), but not the
  express FRCP 26(b)(1) "proportional to the needs of the case" reform.
- **CR 36 deemed-admission timing is still 30 days** (40 days for a defendant served
  with the summons and complaint), subject to the court's discretion to shorten/enlarge.
- **CR 26's mandatory meet-and-confer is CR 26(i)**, not CR 26(f). CR 26(f) is a
  discretionary "Discovery Conference" the court may order; it is **not** a mandatory
  FRCP 26(f) planning conference.

## CR 26 — General Provisions Governing Discovery

**Citation:** Wash. Super. Ct. Civ. R. 26 (CR 26). Amendment history per official
text: amended effective July 1, 1972; Sept. 1, 1985; Sept. 1, 1989; Dec. 28, 1990;
Sept. 1, 1992; Sept. 17, 1993; Sept. 1, 1995; Jan. 12, 2010; Apr. 28, 2015;
July 9, 2024; **Oct. 1, 2024**.

**2024 amendment (major):** Supreme Court Order No. 25700-A-1592 (dated Sept. 5,
2024), adopted under GR 9(j)(1), effective on publication — recognized as
**effective Oct. 1, 2024** (Westlaw "Effective: October 1, 2024"). Key changes:

- **CR 26(b)(5) (experts):** tightened testifying-expert disclosure; parties can no
  longer tactically withhold expert identity/opinions until a case-schedule deadline.
- **CR 26(e) (supplementation):** now a **self-executing duty** to seasonably
  supplement/correct *all* discovery responses (previously limited circumstances).
- **CR 26(g) (signing/objections):** **general objections are now expressly
  prohibited**; a **privilege log is required** for every assertion of privilege
  (objections must be specific to the request; privilege objections must describe
  grounds and identify withheld matters sufficiently to allow evaluation).

**Subsection confirmations:**

- **CR 26(a) — "Discovery Methods."** Lists depositions (oral/written), interrogatories,
  production/entry on land, physical & mental exams, and requests for admission. **WA
  has NO mandatory initial disclosures** (no FRCP 26(a)(1) analog). *Confirmed.*
- **CR 26(b) — Scope and limits.** CR 26(b)(1): "Parties may obtain discovery
  regarding any matter, not privileged, which is relevant to the subject matter
  involved in the pending action." **No express 2015 proportionality standard.**
  CR 26(b)(1)(A)–(C) authorizes the court to limit discovery that is unreasonably
  cumulative/duplicative, obtainable more conveniently elsewhere, where the party had
  ample opportunity, or where burden/expense outweighs likely benefit
  (pre-2015-federal-style limits). *Confirmed.*
- **CR 26(c) — Protective orders.** Court, for good cause, may issue any order justice
  requires to protect from annoyance, embarrassment, oppression, or undue burden/
  expense. Denial → CR 37(a)(4) expense provisions apply. *Confirmed.*
- **CR 26(e) — Duty to supplement.** Self-executing duty to seasonably supplement/
  correct (post-2024). *Confirmed.*
- **CR 26(f) — Discovery Conference.** The court **may** direct a discovery conference,
  and **shall** on a party's motion that includes the required statements (issues,
  proposed plan/schedule, proposed limitations, other proposed orders, and a
  statement of reasonable effort to reach agreement). This is **discretionary**, not
  a mandatory FRCP-26(f)-style planning conference. **The mandatory pre-motion
  meet-and-confer for discovery motions is CR 26(i)** (certification required for
  motions to compel / for protection). *Confirmed — WA does not require a mandatory
  Rule 26(f) conference; the confer-obligation lives in CR 26(i).*

## CR 30 — Depositions Upon Oral Examination

**Citation:** Wash. Super. Ct. Civ. R. 30 (CR 30). History: adopted eff. July 1,
1967; amended July 1, 1972; Apr. 2, 1979; Sept. 1, 1985; Sept. 1, 1988; Sept. 1,
1989; Sept. 1, 1993; Sept. 1, 2005; Apr. 28, 2015; Sept. 1, 2019; Feb. 1, 2021;
**Oct. 1, 2024**.

**2024 amendment (major):** Effective **Oct. 1, 2024** (BJA Remote Proceedings Work
Group). Codifies **depositions by remote means**:
- Remote depositions may be **noticed without leave of court**; deposition proceeds
  as noticed unless, **within three days** of receiving notice, an objecting party or
  the deponent files a motion objecting (court weighs listed factors, including
  whether the mode serves CR 1's just/speedy/inexpensive purpose).
- Integrity provisions (in the remote-deposition subsection): everyone in the room
  with the deponent must remain **audible and visible** for the entire deposition; the
  witness may not refer to notes or electronic communications; **no one may attempt to
  influence** the deponent's answer by any means (notes, text, e-mail, chat, gestures).

**Note on subsection numbering:** The 2024-adopted rule places remote-deposition
provisions in a numbered subsection; secondary sources cite them variously (e.g.,
"CR 30(h)(7)" in the WSBA article vs. "30(b)(7)" in drafting materials). Cite to the
current official CR 30 PDF for the exact letter/number.

## CR 33 — Interrogatories to Parties

**Citation:** Wash. Super. Ct. Civ. R. 33 (CR 33).

**Numerical limit — PREMISE CORRECTION:** The stated premise "WA CR 33 has a 25
limit" is **INCORRECT**. WA CR 33 imposes **no statewide numerical limit** on the
number of interrogatories. (The 25-interrogatory cap "including discrete subparts" is
the *federal* rule, FRCP 33(a)(1).) In Washington, numerical limits are set by
**county local rules**, and they vary:
- King County LCR 26: 40 interrogatories including all discrete subparts (or 15 in
  addition to court-approved pattern interrogatories).
- Pierce County: ~25 (expedited) / 35 (standard); subparts counted separately.
- Snohomish County: 40 including discrete subparts.
- Other counties: verify the specific LCR.

**Response timing (statewide CR 33):** answers/objections due **within 30 days**
after service; a **defendant** may serve within **40 days** after service of the
summons and complaint. Each interrogatory answered separately and fully under oath;
objections stated in lieu of answer; may relate to any matter under CR 26(b).

**Discrete subparts:** Because WA's cap is local, whether "discrete subparts" count
is governed by the applicable **local rule** and case-track — confirm per county.

## CR 34 — Producing Documents, Electronically Stored Information (ESI), and Things; Entry Onto Land

**Citation:** Wash. Super. Ct. Civ. R. 34 (CR 34). History: adopted eff. July 1,
1967; amended July 1, 1972; Sept. 1, 1985; Sept. 1, 1989; Sept. 1, 1997;
**Sept. 1, 2013** (ESI amendment — introduced "electronically stored information" and
form-of-production framework). No 2024–2026 amendment identified.

**Key points:**
- **Particularity:** the request "shall set forth the items to be produced either by
  individual item or by category, and describe each item and category with reasonable
  particularity"; shall specify a reasonable time, place, and manner.
- **Form of ESI:** requesting party **may** specify the form(s) of ESI production. If
  no form is specified or the responding party objects, the producing party must
  produce ESI in a form "in which it is ordinarily maintained" **or** in a "reasonably
  usable form"; need not produce the same ESI in more than one form.
- **Responses/organization:** produce documents/ESI as kept in the usual course of
  business, or organize and label to correspond to the request's categories.

## CR 36 — Requests for Admission

**Citation:** Wash. Super. Ct. Civ. R. 36 (CR 36). Last substantive amendment
effective **Sept. 1, 2015** (per WSR 15-09-016; gender-neutral/style revisions). No
2024–2026 amendment identified.

**Key points (confirmed against official text):**
- **Separately stated:** "Each matter of which an admission is requested shall be
  separately set forth."
- **Deemed-admission timing — still 30 days:** "The matter is admitted unless, within
  **30 days** after service of the request, or within such shorter or longer time as
  the court may allow," the party serves a written answer or objection. A **defendant**
  need not serve answers/objections before **40 days** after service of the summons and
  complaint (unless the court shortens). *Confirmed 30 days (not changed).*
- **Admit / deny / qualify:** answer must specifically admit or deny, or detail why the
  party cannot truthfully admit/deny; a denial must fairly meet the substance; a party
  must qualify/admit-in-part when good faith requires; lack of knowledge only after
  reasonable inquiry.
- **Withdrawal/amendment:** under CR 36(b), an admission is conclusively established
  unless the court permits withdrawal or amendment; courts may also extend the CR 36(a)
  response time even after the 30-day deadline (case law: *Santos v. Dean*).

## CR 37 — Failure to Make Discovery; Sanctions

**Citation:** Wash. Super. Ct. Civ. R. 37 (CR 37). History: adopted eff. July 1,
1967; amended July 1, 1972; Sept. 1, 1985; Sept. 1, 1992; Sept. 1, 1993;
**Apr. 28, 2015**. No 2024–2026 amendment identified.

**Key points:**
- **Motion to compel — CR 37(a):** available on reasonable notice **and upon a showing
  of compliance with CR 26(i)** (the mandatory meet-and-confer). Covers failure to
  answer deposition questions (CR 30/31), failure to designate (CR 30(b)(6)/31(a)),
  failure to answer interrogatories (CR 33), and failure to permit inspection (CR 34).
- **Evasive/incomplete = failure:** CR 37(a)(3) treats an evasive or incomplete answer
  as a failure to answer.
- **Expenses — CR 37(a)(4):** fee-shifting for the motion (can run against the moving
  party if the motion is denied) absent substantial justification.
- **Sanctions for disobeying an order — CR 37(b):** establishing facts, precluding
  claims/defenses/evidence, striking pleadings, staying, dismissal, default judgment,
  contempt (in deposition-forum county).

## CR 45 — Subpoena (to Non-Parties)

**Citation:** Wash. Super. Ct. Civ. R. 45 (CR 45). History: amended July 1, 1967;
July 1, 1972; Sept. 1, 1983; Sept. 1, 1993; Sept. 1, 2007; Jan. 12, 2010;
**July 9, 2024**.

**2024 amendment:** Effective **July 9, 2024** — updated for remote-means testimony
(subpoena must state if testimony is by remote means; deposition subpoena states
method of recording and in-person vs. remote; 5-day written notice if the person
lacks adequate remote-access technology).

**Key points:**
- **Document-only subpoena:** must be served on **each party** per CR 5(b) **at least
  5 days before** service on the non-party (unless parties agree / court orders on good
  cause).
- **Objection window:** a person commanded to produce may serve written objection
  **within 14 days** of service (or before the compliance date if sooner); production
  then only by court order.
- **Undue-burden protection / quash-or-modify** grounds (CR 45(c)); privilege/
  trial-prep protections and clawback (CR 45(d)); place-of-examination limits
  (CR 45(e)(2)); contempt for noncompliance (CR 45(g)).

## "Superior Court Civil Rules (SCR)" — statewide vs. per-county

**Confirmed:** Washington has a **statewide** civil rule set for superior courts,
officially the **Superior Court Civil Rules (CR)**, adopted by the Washington Supreme
Court and applicable in all superior courts. (Note the abbreviation is **"CR,"** not
"SCR" — "SCR" is not the standard WA label; using "SCR" risks confusion.) Each county
superior court additionally maintains **Local Civil Rules (LCR)** that supplement the
statewide CR (case schedules, page limits, and — importantly for discovery —
**numerical limits on interrogatories, RFAs, and depositions**). **Local rules
control over a conflicting statewide/federal default within their proper scope**, so
practitioners must consult both the statewide CR and the venue's LCR.

## Answers to the specific questions posed

- **Did WA amend any discovery rules in 2024–2026?** Yes — in **2024**: **CR 45**
  (eff. July 9, 2024), **CR 26** (eff. Oct. 1, 2024), and **CR 30** (eff. Oct. 1,
  2024). No amendments to the core discovery rules (CR 26–37, 45) took effect in
  **2025 or 2026** as of this review (WA rule text current through ~March 1, 2026).
  (A separate **criminal** discovery rule, CrR/CrRLJ 4.7, had proposed amendments out
  for comment in early 2026 — not a civil discovery rule, out of scope.)
- **Is there a proportionality standard in CR 26(b)(1) (post-2014 reform)?** **No** —
  WA has **not** adopted the federal 2015 "proportional to the needs of the case"
  amendment. CR 26(b)(1) still uses the broader "relevant to the subject matter"
  scope, with court-imposed limits in CR 26(b)(1)(A)–(C) (the pre-2015-federal-style
  cumulative/duplicative, ample-opportunity, and burden-vs-benefit factors).
- **Is the CR 33 interrogatory limit still 25?** **It was never 25 under WA CR 33.**
  There is **no statewide numerical limit**; limits are set by county local rules
  (commonly 40 in King/Snohomish; ~25/35 by track in Pierce). Confirm the venue's LCR.
- **Is CR 36 deemed admission still 30 days or different?** **Still 30 days** after
  service (40 days for a defendant served with the summons and complaint), subject to
  the court's discretion to shorten or enlarge.
