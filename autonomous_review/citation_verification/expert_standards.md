## 1. Federal — FRE 702 / Daubert

### FRE 702 — current text (verified against the official Dec. 1, 2024 edition, uscourts.gov, and govinfo.gov)

**Amendment history clarification:** The request called the 2023 change a "restyling." That is imprecise — the FRE were *restyled* in 2011; the **December 1, 2023 change was a substantive amendment** (approved by the Supreme Court Apr. 24, 2023, effective Dec. 1, 2023). Verified current text:

> A witness who is qualified as an expert by knowledge, skill, experience, training, or education may testify in the form of an opinion or otherwise if the proponent demonstrates to the court that it is more likely than not that:
>
> (a) the expert's scientific, technical, or other specialized knowledge will help the trier of fact to understand the evidence or to determine a fact in issue;
> (b) the testimony is based on sufficient facts or data;
> (c) the testimony is the product of reliable principles and methods; and
> (d) the expert's opinion reflects a reliable application of the principles and methods to the facts of the case.

The 2023 amendment made two changes (per the Advisory Committee Note):
1. Added the stem language "if the proponent demonstrates to the court that it is more likely than not" — codifying the Rule 104(a) preponderance standard for all four admissibility elements and rejecting case law treating sufficiency-of-basis and application-of-methodology as "weight, not admissibility."
2. Rewrote subsection (d) from "the expert has reliably applied the principles and methods" to "the expert's opinion reflects a reliable application of the principles and methods" — the opinion must stay within what the basis and methodology can reliably support.

**No 2024-2026 amendment to Rule 702.** Verified against (a) the official Federal Rules of Evidence pamphlet "as amended to December 1, 2024" (uscourts.gov, which lists the Dec. 2024 amendments as affecting other rules, not 702), and (b) 2025-2026 practitioner and law-review commentary (Univ. of Cincinnati L. Rev. 2026 "Early Returns" article; IADC Oct. 2025 supplement) treating the 2023 text as current. `needs_attorney_rule_confirm=false`

### Daubert / Kumho

- **Daubert v. Merrell Dow Pharmaceuticals, Inc., 509 U.S. 579 (1993)** — held FRE 702 (not Frye) governs in federal court; trial judge is gatekeeper for relevance and reliability. Good law; the 2023 amendment reinforces, not displaces, Daubert gatekeeping. `needs_attorney_rule_confirm=false`
- **Kumho Tire Co. v. Carmichael, 526 U.S. 137 (1999)** — Daubert gatekeeping applies to *all* expert testimony (technical and other specialized knowledge, e.g., engineering), not just "scientific" testimony; factors are flexible. Good law. `needs_attorney_rule_confirm=false`
- General Electric Co. v. Joiner, 522 U.S. 136 (1997) (abuse-of-discretion review; "analytical gap") is the third case in the trilogy and worth including in the skill.

## 2. California — Kelly/Frye + Sargon + Sanchez (NOT Daubert)

California **does not follow Daubert** in state court. Two distinct gates coexist:

1. **Kelly (Kelly/Frye) general acceptance** — applies only to *novel scientific techniques/methods of proof*.
2. **Sargon gatekeeping under Evid. Code §§ 801(b) & 802** — applies to *all* expert opinion (speculation, unsupported leaps).

### People v. Kelly (1976) 17 Cal.3d 24 — VERIFIED, still good law

Adopted Frye's general-acceptance test for novel scientific techniques. Expressly reaffirmed post-Daubert in **People v. Leahy (1994) 8 Cal.4th 587** (declining to adopt Daubert; "Kelly/Frye" now more accurately the "Kelly" test). *Sargon* (2012) footnote 6 expressly preserved Kelly/Leahy ("Nothing we say in this case affects our holding in Leahy regarding new scientific techniques"). Appellate courts continued applying Kelly through at least 2022 (*Bader v. Johnson & Johnson* (2022), holding a Sargon objection does not preserve a Kelly objection). No indication of abrogation through 2026. `needs_attorney_rule_confirm=false`

### Sargon Enterprises, Inc. v. University of Southern California (2012) 55 Cal.4th 747 — VERIFIED, still good law

**Correct citation: 55 Cal.4th 747** (the request's "53 Cal.4th 1210" is wrong — do not use it). Under Evid. Code §§ 801(b) and 802, trial courts have a duty to act as gatekeepers to exclude expert opinion that is (1) based on matter of a type an expert may not reasonably rely on, (2) based on reasons unsupported by the material relied upon, or (3) speculative. The court "may inquire into, not only the type of material on which an expert relies, but also whether that material actually supports the expert's reasoning." Sargon addresses reasoning/speculation; it is *not* a general-acceptance test (Kelly still governs novelty). `needs_attorney_rule_confirm=false`

### People v. Sanchez (2016) 63 Cal.4th 665 — VERIFIED, still good law

**Correct citation: 63 Cal.4th 665** (the request's "1 Cal.5th 865" is wrong — do not use it). An expert may not relate **case-specific facts** asserted in hearsay statements as true unless independently proven by competent evidence or covered by a hearsay exception; limiting instructions cannot cure this. Experts may still rely on hearsay in forming opinions and may testify to general background knowledge from training and experience. Applied in civil as well as criminal cases.

Verified progeny (California Supreme Court): **People v. Veamatahau (2020) 9 Cal.5th 16** (drug-database testimony was background, not case-specific hearsay) and **People v. Perez (2020) 9 Cal.5th 1** (no forfeiture for pre-Sanchez failure to object). *(Reporter volume/page for Veamatahau and Perez taken from standard citation practice; docket numbers S249872 and S248730 verified — spot-check the reporter cites before filing.)* **2024-2026 progeny:** none identified in this verification pass; a dedicated Westlaw/Lexis KeyCite check is required before relying on any specific post-2023 Sanchez decision. `needs_attorney_rule_confirm=true` **(for 2024-2026 progeny only; the core Sanchez holding is verified good law)**

## 3. Washington — Frye + ER 702 (NOT Daubert)

### Current standard — VERIFIED as of March 2026

Washington **still applies Frye** for novel scientific evidence, layered with ER 702 for all expert testimony. This was applied by Division III of the Washington Court of Appeals in an opinion **filed March 5, 2026** (No. 40095-6-III, FELA diesel-exhaust case), which restated the framework: novel scientific evidence must satisfy **both Frye and ER 702** (citing *State v. Copeland*, 130 Wn.2d 244, 255-56 (1996)); where methodology is long-accepted and not novel, Frye does not apply and ER 702 alone governs.

**Premise corrections:**
- The controlling retention case is **State v. Copeland, 130 Wn.2d 244, 922 P.2d 1304 (1996)** — the Washington Supreme Court expressly considered and **rejected Daubert**, holding "Frye remains the standard for admissibility of novel scientific evidence in Washington." There is no "State v. Frye (1995)" and no "State v. Copeland-Bryant (2018)" that could be located. `needs_attorney_rule_confirm=true` for "Copeland-Bryant" (treat as nonexistent; use Copeland).
- **GR 37 is Washington's General Rule on peremptory challenges/jury selection** — it has nothing to do with expert evidence. "ERI" is not a Washington evidence rule. The request's "superseded by GR 37 / ERI" premise is false; Frye has not been superseded.

Supporting cases (verified via published sources):
- **State v. Cauthron, 120 Wn.2d 879 (1993)** — two-part ER 702 test (qualified expert; helpful to trier of fact).
- **Anderson v. Akzo Nobel Coatings, Inc., 172 Wn.2d 593, 260 P.3d 857 (2011)** — Frye applies in civil cases; general acceptance required for both the underlying theory and the techniques/experiments/studies applying it, which must be capable of producing reliable results.
- **L.M. v. Hamilton, 193 Wn.2d 113, 436 P.3d 803 (2019)** — Frye articulation; application of accepted techniques to reach novel conclusions does not trigger Frye; exclusion only if significant dispute among qualified scientists.
- **State v. Cheatam, 150 Wn.2d 626 (2003)** — reaffirmed Frye commitment.

`needs_attorney_rule_confirm=false` for the Frye + ER 702 framework itself.

### ER 702 — current text (verified from courts.wa.gov official PDF)

> **ER 702 TESTIMONY BY EXPERTS.** If scientific, technical, or other specialized knowledge will assist the trier of fact to understand the evidence or to determine a fact in issue, a witness qualified as an expert by knowledge, skill, experience, training, or education, may testify thereto in the form of an opinion or otherwise.
>
> [Adopted effective April 2, 1979.]

**ER 702 does NOT track the 2023 FRE 702 amendment.** It remains the pre-Daubert 1979 text (mirroring pre-2000 FRE 702) with no subsections (a)-(d), no "sufficient facts or data" clause, and no preponderance stem. The 2025 and 2026 editions of the Washington Rules of Evidence show no amendment to ER 702, 703, 704, or 705. `needs_attorney_rule_confirm=false`

### ER 703 / 704 / 705 (titles verified from courts.wa.gov rule list; operative text summarized — spot-check full text before quoting verbatim)

- **ER 703 — Bases of Opinion Testimony by Experts:** facts or data may be those perceived by or made known to the expert at or before the hearing; if of a type reasonably relied upon by experts in the field, they need not be admissible in evidence.
- **ER 704 — Opinion on Ultimate Issue:** opinion otherwise admissible is not objectionable because it embraces an ultimate issue to be decided by the trier of fact.
- **ER 705 — Disclosure of Facts or Data Underlying Expert Opinion:** the expert may give an opinion without first testifying to underlying facts or data, unless the court requires otherwise; underlying facts/data may be required on cross-examination.

No recent amendments found. Because the verbatim texts of 703-705 were verified only by title and secondary summary in this pass, mark verbatim quoting: `needs_attorney_rule_confirm=true` (verbatim text only; existence and substance verified).

## 4. Daubert Factors — and the different gates by jurisdiction

The non-exclusive Daubert reliability factors (509 U.S. at 593-94):

1. **Testability** — whether the theory or technique can be (and has been) tested / falsified.
2. **Peer review and publication** — whether it has been subjected to peer review.
3. **Known or potential error rate** — of the technique when applied.
4. **Standards and controls** — existence and maintenance of standards controlling the technique's operation.
5. **General acceptance** — within the relevant scientific community (a factor, not the test).

Kumho Tire: the factors are flexible and apply (as fitting) to all expert testimony; Joiner: too great an "analytical gap" between data and opinion is excludable.

**Different gates — critical for the skill's jurisdiction routing:**

| Forum | Novel-science gate | General expert gate |
|---|---|---|
| Federal | FRE 702 / Daubert (all factors; preponderance per 2023 amendment) | Same — FRE 702(a)-(d) |
| California | **Kelly** general acceptance (novel scientific techniques only) | §§ 720, 801, 802 + **Sargon** anti-speculation gatekeeping + **Sanchez** hearsay-basis limits + **§ 801.1** for medical causation |
| Washington | **Frye** general acceptance (novel scientific theory AND its techniques; both Frye and ER 702 must be met) | ER 702 two-part test (qualified; helpful to trier of fact) — a materially lower bar than amended FRE 702 |

Practical consequence: an expert theory that survives in WA/CA state court under general acceptance may still fail federal Daubert scrutiny (and vice versa — a non-novel but weakly-reasoned opinion faces a tougher gate federally, and in CA under Sargon, than under WA's ER 702). Removal to federal court changes the gate.

## 5. Foundation for Plaintiff Liability Experts

*General practice guidance synthesized from the verified standards above — not itself a statute or holding.* `needs_attorney_rule_confirm=true` (as applied practice guidance; have supervising attorney confirm per-matter)

For each liability discipline, the foundation must satisfy the forum's gate (Section 4) plus:

- **Accident reconstruction:** qualification (ACTAR accreditation, engineering degree, or law-enforcement reconstruction training per § 720 / ER 702 / FRE 702); reliance on scene data, photogrammetry, EDR/black-box downloads, physical evidence (momentum/energy methods are long-accepted, so Kelly/Frye typically not triggered — but novel simulation software or techniques may be); in CA, Sanchez limits reciting case-specific hearsay (e.g., witness statements in a police report) unless independently admitted; federally, post-2023 FRE 702(d) demands the reconstruction opinion not outrun the physical inputs.
- **Human factors:** qualification in psychology/ergonomics; peer-reviewed perception-reaction literature; opinions tied to case-specific measured conditions (sight lines, lighting, conspicuity); vulnerable to Sargon/702(d) challenge if the expert generalizes from literature to this plaintiff without case-specific anchoring.
- **Safety / industry standards:** familiarity with the governing standards (ANSI, OSHA/WISHA, ASTM, building codes, industry custom) and their applicability to the defendant's conduct at the relevant time; standards themselves usually enter as basis material (ER 703 / § 801(b) / FRE 703); ultimate-issue opinions permissible (ER 704; CA Evid. Code § 805) but avoid pure legal conclusions.
- **Engineering (product defect, premises):** licensure/discipline match to the defect theory; testing or accepted analytical methods (FEA, materials analysis); novel testing protocols can trigger Kelly (CA) or Frye (WA); federally, alternative-design opinions must reflect reliable application to the actual product (702(d)).

## 6. Foundation for Plaintiff Damages Experts

*Same caveat as Section 5.* `needs_attorney_rule_confirm=true` (as applied practice guidance)

- **Medical experts / causation:** licensure and specialty match; causation to a **reasonable medical probability** (CA: Kline line, and note **§ 801.1** now holds defense alternative-cause experts to the same probability standard — a plaintiff-side exclusion tool operative Jan. 1, 2024); Sanchez governs reciting other providers' records in CA (records should come in via hearsay exception — e.g., business records — or the expert testifies in general terms/hypotheticals); differential diagnosis is generally accepted methodology but its *application* is attackable under amended FRE 702(d) and WA's ER 702 (see the March 2026 WA FELA opinion excluding a causation expert under ER 702 while finding Frye inapplicable).
- **Forensic economists:** accepted methods (present-value discounting, worklife expectancy tables, wage-growth data); inputs must be case-specific (actual earnings history, vocational findings); classic Sargon territory — *Sargon itself excluded a speculative lost-profits economic opinion*; federally, 702(b) "sufficient facts or data" is the pressure point.
- **Vocational rehabilitation:** CRC/CDMS-type credentialing; standard methodologies (transferable-skills analysis, labor-market surveys); must connect medical restrictions (from a physician, not self-generated) to employability — chaining opinions requires the underlying medical opinion to be independently admissible.
- **Life care planners:** CLCP-type credentialing plus collaboration with treating/consulting physicians; each item of future care should trace to a medical recommendation (avoids Sanchez recitation problems and Sargon speculation attacks); costing from recognized databases satisfies § 801(b) / ER 703 reasonable-reliance.
